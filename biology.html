Cell Biology — Introduction

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms are made up of one or more cells.


Cells can be broadly classified into prokaryotic cells and eukaryotic cells. Plant and animal cells are examples of eukaryotic cells.


Important cell structures include the nucleus, cell membrane, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes, and other specialized organelles.

Human Biology — Introduction

Human biology focuses on the structure and functions of the human body.


Major body systems include the digestive system, respiratory system, circulatory system, nervous system, reproductive system, and excretory system.

Ecology — Introduction

Ecology is the study of how living organisms interact with one another and with their physical environment.


An ecosystem consists of living components, known as biotic factors, and non-living components, known as abiotic factors.

Genetics — Introduction

Genetics is the study of heredity and variation in living organisms.


Genes are units of heredity found on chromosomes. They carry information that influences inherited characteristics.

Plant Biology — Introduction

Plants are organisms that can produce their own food through photosynthesis.


Photosynthesis uses light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose, releasing oxygen as a by-product.

Microbiology — Introduction

Microbiology is the study of microscopic organisms such as bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and viruses.


Microorganisms can be beneficial or harmful. They play important roles in medicine, agriculture, food production, and ecosystems.
